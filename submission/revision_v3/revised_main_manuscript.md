## ABSTRACT

Background: Health care organizations increasingly deploy artificial intelligence (AI) systems for medical triage, yet systematic comparisons of architectural paradigms on real-world patient communications remain limited. Most validation studies rely on physician-created vignettes that underrepresent the colloquial language, abbreviations, and implicit context of actual patient messages.

Objective: We compared nine AI configurations across four architectural paradigms—decision-theoretic controllers, constellation architectures, local supervised classifiers, and large language models (LLMs)—on clinical safety hazard detection and triage action appropriateness using both physician-created scenarios and authentic patient messages from a Medicaid population health program.

Methods: This retrospective evaluation compared nine configurations on two held-out test sets: 41 physician-created triage scenarios (61% hazard prevalence) and 2,000 real-world patient messages from a multistate Medicaid program (January 2023–November 2025; 8.25% hazard prevalence; 165 hazardous messages). Three board-certified physicians established ground truth (Fleiss kappa=0.82 for hazard presence). The nine configurations were: a conservative Q-learning decision controller (two threshold variants—sensitivity-optimized and reward-optimized), constellation architecture, rule-based guardrails, logistic regression with TF-IDF features, XGBoost with sentence embeddings, GPT-5.1 with safety-augmented and default system prompts, and fine-tuned Llama-1.1B. All nine architectures were evaluated on identical test cases; pairwise comparisons used McNemar tests with Hochberg step-up procedure for FWER control (FWER ≤.05; equivalent to Bonferroni for all comparisons in this analysis). DeepSeek-R1 was additionally evaluated on the complete 2,000-message test set. The study was determined exempt by WCG IRB (tracking ID: 20253751).

Results: Real-world messages differed substantially from physician scenarios: higher rates of colloquialisms (1,410/3,000, 47% vs 15/200, 8%), abbreviations (930/3,000, 31% vs 24/200, 12%), implicit context (690/3,000, 23% vs 9/200, 5%), and lower reading level (grade 6.2 vs grade 11.4; all P<.001). On the real-world 2,000-message test set, the CQL controller (sensitivity-optimized) achieved the highest sensitivity (0.727; 95% CI 0.655–0.789; PPV 0.194; false negatives 22.5 per 1,000 messages; F1 0.306); however, this reflected asymmetric reward-threshold optimization rather than superior overall discrimination: the constellation architecture and rule-based guardrails led all nine configurations by AUROC (0.801 each; controller AUROC 0.731). The safety-augmented GPT-5.1 achieved sensitivity 0.400 and AUROC 0.651; logistic regression with TF-IDF matched it at default threshold (sensitivity 0.394; McNemar P>.99) while achieving higher AUROC (0.716 vs 0.651). Fine-tuned Llama-1.1B achieved AUROC 0.701, exceeding both GPT-5.1 configurations. At matched specificity (0.73), local supervised architectures exceeded LLMs by 30–35 percentage points in sensitivity. The action recommendation component achieved 77.7% appropriate actions (95% CI 75.8%–79.5%) versus 15.6%–16.4% for LLMs. Among patients with available demographic data (77%), sensitivity was 0.753 for female and 0.783 for male patients (equalized odds difference 0.047). DeepSeek-R1, evaluated on the complete 2,000-message test set, achieved sensitivity 0.224 (37/165 hazards detected; 95% CI 0.163–0.290), comparable to GPT-5.1 configurations at similar operating points.

Conclusions: In this formative single-program retrospective internal validation, local supervised architectures substantially outperformed tested LLM configurations, exceeding zero-shot LLMs by 30–35 percentage points in sensitivity at matched operating points. Performance leadership was metric-specific: the decision-theoretic controller led on sensitivity, while constellation and guardrail architectures led on overall discrimination. Logistic regression matched GPT-5.1 sensitivity and outperformed it on AUROC, and fine-tuning improved LLM discrimination—suggesting that supervised calibration and optimization pressure, more than architectural category per se, drive the performance gap. Real-world validation using authentic patient communications should be standard practice before deploying AI triage systems.

## INTRODUCTION

Health care organizations face increasing demand for asynchronous patient communication through digital messaging platforms [1,2]. While AI systems promise to scale triage capacity, ensuring patient safety requires distinguishing emergencies requiring immediate escalation from routine concerns suitable for standard care pathways [3,4]. The rapid deployment of large language models (LLMs) in clinical workflows [5,6] has generated both enthusiasm for their potential [7] and concern about safety performance when errors carry significant clinical consequences [8-10].

Recent evidence highlights persistent safety concerns with LLM-based clinical decision support. Evaluation of retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems on complex medical queries found that even with extensive retrieval from medical literature, challenges remain in evidence selection and factual accuracy [11]. Assessment of a medical RAG-based search engine on subspecialty board examination questions revealed only 31%-41% accuracy with 72%-77% repeatability [12], raising concerns about deployment without adequate validation. Among patient cases requiring specialist consultation, LLMs frequently recommended inappropriate actions despite access to full clinical context [13], while systematic reviews document inconsistent performance on clinical reasoning tasks with particular vulnerability to edge cases [14,15]. LLM deployment without adequate safety evaluation has raised concerns from multiple medical informatics experts [7,16,19], yet which architectural approaches best ensure patient safety remains incompletely characterized.

### Knowledge Gaps

Existing literature reveals three gaps. First, systematic comparisons of architectural paradigms—from unassisted generative models to formal decision-theoretic controllers—on identical datasets remain limited. Prior work either compares different LLMs within the same architectural category [17] or evaluates traditional machine learning algorithms in specific clinical tasks without systematic cross-paradigm comparison [16]. This fragmentation limits evidence-based selection of appropriate architectures for safety-critical applications.

Second, evaluation using physician-created clinical vignettes rather than authentic patient communications remains prevalent [7,18,20]. Standard medical AI benchmarks including MedQA (US Medical Licensing Examination questions) [20] and medical domain evaluations [18,35] rely on professionally authored examination questions rather than real patient language. Landmark comparative evaluations of AI clinical reasoning have used standardized patient consultations (typically N=50-300) or curated vignette sets rather than large corpora of de-identified messages from routine care operations [13,17]. Systems optimized on synthetic scenarios may not generalize to production environments where patients use colloquial language, abbreviations, and implicit contextual references.

Third, evaluation focuses predominantly on diagnostic accuracy rather than safety-specific metrics [7,16]. High overall accuracy can mask patterns of false negatives for rare but life-threatening conditions. Emergency triage demands explicit measurement of sensitivity for hazard detection (true positive rate), F1 score (harmonic mean of precision and recall), specificity (true negative rate), and positive predictive value (precision) to characterize the full operating profile.

### Current Architectural Landscape

Recent deployments highlight architectural diversity. We implemented a multimodel "constellation" architecture using multiple specialized classifiers for category-specific prediction, applying this design to complex clinical classification tasks requiring domain decomposition (see Methods below and Multimedia Appendix 1, section A). Retrieval-augmented generation from continuously updated medical literature supports clinical decision-making, following foundational retrieval-augmented generation methods [11,22,25]. Traditional machine learning approaches demonstrate strong predictive performance when properly calibrated: gradient boosting and logistic regression models have outperformed prompted generative models on structured clinical prediction tasks in multiple independent evaluations, including ICU transfer prediction and emergency department triage, as reviewed in Wornow et al. [16] and demonstrated in AI Clinician for critical care [26]. Decision-theoretic approaches using offline reinforcement learning show promise for critical care resource allocation [27,28,39], providing mathematical frameworks while avoiding safety risks of online exploration.

### Study Objectives

As a systematic evaluation to characterize architectural tradeoffs and inform evidence-based architecture selection for clinical deployment, we evaluated four architectural paradigms—decision-theoretic controllers, constellation architectures, local supervised classifiers, and large language models—in nine total configurations on both physician-created test scenarios and real-world patient messages from a Medicaid population health program. We hypothesized that (1) architectures with explicit safety constraints would demonstrate superior sensitivity compared to unassisted generative approaches, and (2) performance reduction from synthetic to real-world data would be greater for architectures lacking domain-specific supervised training. We additionally examined whether performance leadership was consistent across metrics (sensitivity, AUROC, action accuracy) or metric-specific. This evaluation is distinguished from prior AI triage validation studies by: (1) the use of 3,000 authentic patient messages rather than physician-created vignettes, enabling assessment under real deployment conditions; (2) a Medicaid population health program setting, which addresses a representativeness gap in AI safety evaluations dominated by academic medical center cohorts; (3) natural clinical hazard prevalence (8.25%) rather than enriched benchmark prevalence; and (4) systematic head-to-head comparison across all four major architectural paradigms on identical test cases.

## METHODS

### Study Design and Oversight

We conducted a retrospective evaluation study comparing AI architecture performance on medical triage safety hazard detection.

### Ethical Considerations

This study was determined exempt from full review by WCG IRB (Princeton, New Jersey; tracking ID: 20253751) under 45 CFR 46.104(d)(4) as secondary research using existing deidentified data. As a retrospective evaluation of deidentified message data, individual informed consent was waived by the IRB. All patient messages were deidentified in accordance with HIPAA Safe Harbor standards prior to analysis; no direct patient identifiers (names, dates of birth, geographic identifiers, or account numbers) were retained in the analysis dataset. No compensation was provided to patients or participants, as this study involved only analysis of retrospective operational data without prospective enrollment. No identifiable images of individual participants appear in this manuscript or supplementary materials.

### Data Sources and Ground Truth Establishment

We compared architectures on two held-out test datasets: physician-created clinical scenarios and real-world patient messages.

Physician-created test set (N=41): We developed a total of 200 synthetic triage scenarios written by board-certified physicians to represent common and high-risk presentations across emergency medicine, internal medicine, and family medicine, informed by standardized triage protocols and telephone triage guidelines (see Multimedia Appendix 1 section B). A random sample of 41 scenarios (20.5%) was held out as the physician test set, with the remaining 159 used for training activities detailed below. The physician test set had 61% hazard prevalence (approximately 25 hazard scenarios and 16 benign scenarios), differing substantially from the real-world prevalence (8.25%) and preventing direct cross-set comparison of absolute performance values.

Real-world test set (N=2,000): The real-world dataset comprised 3,000 patient messages from a multistate care management and population health program for patients in Medicaid. Messages included secure text messaging, transcripts of voice-based telephone triage calls, and care coordinator communications from January 2023 through November 2025. A stratified random sample of 2,000 messages was held out as the real-world test set, with the remaining 1,000 used for development activities. Splitting was performed at the message level; of the 1,679 unique test-set patients, 41 (2.4%) also contributed messages to the development set, yielding 68 of 2,000 test messages (3.4%) from patients with development-set data. Since models were trained on aggregate text features (TF-IDF or sentence-BERT embeddings) without patient identifiers, this 3.4% overlap is unlikely to materially affect performance estimates, though patient-level within-message patterns cannot be completely ruled out as a minor source of leakage. Temporal holdout validation (e.g., training on earlier messages and testing on later) was not performed; accordingly, these results reflect internal validation performance rather than prospective deployment performance.

Ground truth establishment: Three board-certified physicians in internal medicine with median 13 years post-residency experience (range 7-14 years) independently reviewed all messages to establish ground truth for: (1) presence of clinical safety hazards requiring escalation, (2) hazard category (23 categories), and (3) appropriate triage action. Disagreements were resolved through structured consensus. Fleiss kappa was 0.82 for hazard presence and 0.76 for hazard category (substantial agreement). Hazard prevalence was 8.25% (165/2,000) in the real-world test set, reflecting natural prevalence in routine care communications.

Linguistic Feature Annotation: Linguistic characteristics were quantified using automated NLP tools with manual validation on a 200-message random sample. Colloquialisms were identified using a domain-specific lexicon of 847 informal terms (e.g., 'feels off', 'real bad', 'gimme') compiled from published health literacy resources and supplemented by inspection of the most common non-standard terms in the corpus; a message was classified as containing colloquialisms if it contained ≥1 colloquial term not in standard medical or general English dictionaries (pyspellchecker v0.7.2). Abbreviations were detected using a combined rule-based approach: messages were classified as containing abbreviations if any token was (a) ≤4 characters, (b) not a stopword or single-letter pronoun, and (c) appeared ≥20× in the corpus or matched a manually curated list of 312 common informal health abbreviations (e.g., 'dr', 'meds', 'appt', 'er'). Implicit contextual references were identified as messages in which the presumed antecedent of a pronoun or nominal reference could not be resolved without prior conversation history, assessed using spaCy v3.5 coreference resolution. Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level was computed using the textstat library (v0.7.0). Manual review of a 200-message random sample confirmed accuracy: colloquialism detection agreement 89%, abbreviation detection 93%, implicit context 81%, with Cohen's kappa 0.73, 0.86, and 0.64 respectively.

### Data Splits and Leakage Prevention

Test sets were held out before any model development and never accessed during training, hyperparameter tuning, or threshold calibration. Temperature scaling was tuned on a 20% held-out calibration subset of the development pool.

### AI Architectures Evaluated

We evaluated four architectural paradigms in nine total configurations (see Multimedia Appendix 1 for complete implementation details):

1–2. Decision-Theoretic Controllers (two variants): This architecture explicitly models medical triage as a sequential decision problem under uncertainty and comprises two components evaluated for different endpoints: (a) a hazard detection component mapping calibrated 23-dimensional hazard probability vectors (from an upstream SBERT+logistic regression classifier with temperature scaling calibration: T=0.548, Expected Calibration Error ECE=0.041) to escalation actions via conservative Q-learning [27]; and (b) an action recommendation component trained as a supervised neural network mapping sentence-BERT embeddings directly to 9 clinical action classes via offline reinforcement learning, achieving highest action appropriateness. We evaluated two threshold variants for the hazard detection component: (1a) the sensitivity-optimized variant (CQL controller, primary), which applies a calibrated probability threshold tuned on the development set to maximize sensitivity subject to a minimum specificity constraint; and (1b) the reward-optimized variant (CQL controller, secondary), which uses the Q-function's argmax action directly without post-hoc threshold adjustment. The conservative Q-learning algorithm prevents overestimation of out-of-distribution actions through explicit policy regularization. The reward function encoded clinical safety priorities: correct escalation +10, missed hazards −50 (reflecting disproportionate clinical harm), unnecessary false alarms −2 (operational burden). This reward structure was specified a priori by clinical consensus to reflect estimated harm ratios; sensitivity analyses varying weights ±50% are reported in Multimedia Appendix 1. State representation: 23-dimensional probability vector over hazard categories from calibrated classifier. Action space: 9 escalation levels from emergency services to self-care.

Action mapping for non-CQL architectures: For architectures that produce binary hazard detection outputs (guardrails, logistic regression, XGBoost), the predicted hazard probability was mapped to the 9-point action space via a deterministic lookup table calibrated on the development set: probability ≥0.85 → Action 0 (emergency services); 0.65–0.85 → Action 1 (urgent physician callback within 1 hour); 0.45–0.65 → Action 2 (same-day appointment); 0.30–0.45 → Action 3 (urgent prescription refill); 0.20–0.30 → Action 4 (urgent specialist referral); 0.10–0.20 → Action 5 (routine follow-up); 0.05–0.10 → Action 6 (routine appointment within 2 weeks); 0.01–0.05 → Action 7 (self-care guidance); <0.01 → Action 8 (no action needed). For the constellation architecture, each category-specific classifier output was used to select both the action level and the specific triage category. For LLM architectures, action classification was performed by a post-processing step that mapped free-text triage recommendations to the nearest of the 9 action classes using semantic similarity to action class descriptions.

3. Constellation Architecture: Explicit modeling of category-specific patterns with per-category calibration. An ensemble of 23 specialized classifiers, each predicting one hazard category using logistic regression on sentence-BERT embeddings [30], with final prediction via weighted voting with confidence thresholds. This architecture inputs sentence-BERT embeddings rather than raw text or calibrated probability vectors, using identical input representation to the local ML baselines.

4. Rule-Based Guardrails: Pattern matching system with 147 hand-crafted rules encoding symptom combinations, temporal keywords, and severity indicators, combined with semantic similarity detection using sentence-BERT embeddings [30] (threshold 0.75). Rules derived from emergency medicine protocols [31] and telephone triage guidelines.

5–6. Classical Machine Learning Baselines (two configurations): We evaluated logistic regression with term frequency-inverse document frequency (TF-IDF) features and XGBoost with sentence-BERT embeddings [30], both trained on a balanced training set of 570 labeled messages: 285 hazard examples (113 real-world development messages confirmed as hazardous plus 132 physician-created hazard scenarios plus 40 additional physician-generated scenarios covering rare but high-severity presentations) paired with 285 benign examples (real-world development messages confirmed as non-hazardous by physician consensus review).

7–9. Large Language Models (three configurations on full 2,000-message test set): We evaluated GPT-5.1 with a safety-augmented system prompt (GPT-5.1 safety), GPT-5.1 with a default (null) system prompt (GPT-5.1 default), and fine-tuned Llama-1.1B. GPT-5.1 was accessed via the OpenAI API (accessed November 2025 through February 2026; temperature parameter is not user-configurable for this model family; inference was deterministic with fixed random seed). The safety-augmented prompt explicitly instructed the model to prioritize patient safety and err toward escalation; the default prompt provided the same task description without safety-prioritization language. Fine-tuned Llama-1.1B [41] was trained via QLoRA on the same 570-example training set used for classical ML baselines. All three LLM configurations used zero-shot prompting (for GPT-5.1 variants) or fine-tuning-only inference (for Llama).

Additional full-test-set evaluation (not one of the nine primary configurations): DeepSeek-R1 [33], deployed locally via Ollama (deepseek-r1:latest), was evaluated on the complete 2,000-message real-world test set using a safety-augmented prompt. Results are reported alongside the few-shot analyses.

Retrieval quality validation: To assess potential vocabulary mismatch between patient messages and formal clinical guidelines, we computed the cosine similarity distribution between queries (patient messages) and their top-1 retrieved passages from a 2.4-million-token clinical guideline corpus. Mean cosine similarity was 0.51 (SD 0.14), compared to 0.73 (SD 0.10) for queries against same-source guideline passages — indicating moderate vocabulary mismatch between informal patient language and formal clinical guidelines. Among the 23% of queries with top-1 similarity < 0.40, RAG augmentation did not improve GPT-5.1 sensitivity versus the base model. This vocabulary gap suggests RAG would provide limited benefit for colloquial patient message classification against formal medical text; full RAG evaluation on the primary test set is identified as a Future Work priority.

Comparability note: The decision-theoretic controller and constellation architecture receive calibrated probability vectors or embeddings from upstream supervised classifiers, representing a multi-stage pipeline with domain-specific feature engineering, category decomposition, and probabilistic calibration. LLM configurations process raw text with prompting or fine-tuning only. Accordingly, observed performance differences reflect the combined advantages of supervised domain-specific feature extraction, calibration, and decision-theoretic optimization — not architecture alone. Future work isolating the contribution of each component is needed.

All models were implemented in Python 3.11.5, PyTorch 2.1.0, Transformers 4.35.2, scikit-learn 1.3.2. Random seed fixed (42). No test set data were used during model training, threshold calibration, hyperparameter selection, or any other development activities.

### Outcome Measures

Primary outcome: Sensitivity for hazard detection on real-world test set at default/calibrated decision thresholds.

Secondary outcomes: Specificity, positive predictive value (PPV), negative predictive value (NPV), F1 score (harmonic mean of PPV and sensitivity), Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC), AUROC, false negatives per 1,000 messages, and operating point analysis at matched specificity levels. Action recommendation appropriateness (proportion of messages receiving appropriate triage level; under-triage and over-triage rates).

### Statistical Analysis

All nine architectures were evaluated on the identical 2,000-message test set, enabling paired comparisons. We compared architectures using McNemar's test for paired binary outcomes [36] with the Hochberg step-up procedure for family-wise error rate control (FWER ≤.05 for 36 pairwise comparisons among 9 configurations) [Hochberg Y. Biometrika. 1988;75(4):800-802]. The Hochberg procedure is uniformly at least as powerful as Bonferroni while maintaining FWER control under positive dependence — the expected condition when all architectures are evaluated on identical cases. The equivalent Bonferroni threshold is α=.0014; the Hochberg step-up procedure additionally permits rejection of hypotheses with larger p-values when sufficient evidence accumulates from smaller p-values in the family. Where Hochberg and Bonferroni reached identical conclusions (which was true for all comparisons in this analysis), we report Hochberg-corrected significance; for equity subgroup analyses, we additionally report Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate correction [Benjamini Y, Hochberg Y. J R Stat Soc B. 1995;57(1):289-300], which is more powerful and appropriate when missing true disparities carries public health risk. Exact binomial tests with Wilson score 95% CIs were used for individual sensitivity and specificity estimates. For F1, PPV, NPV, and action accuracy, we computed 95% CIs using bootstrap resampling (10,000 iterations). For cross-set performance comparisons between physician and real-world test sets, AUROC was used as the primary comparator metric, as AUROC is threshold-independent and substantially less sensitive to prevalence differences than F1 or sensitivity alone; absolute sensitivity comparisons are reported as exploratory given the substantially different prevalence (61% physician vs 8.25% real-world) and sample size (N=41 vs N=2,000). Temporal stability was assessed through a quasi-prospective sensitivity analysis: the 2,000 test messages were sorted lexicographically by CUID identifier (encoding approximate creation timestamp) and divided into chronologically-earlier (first 1,000) and chronologically-later (last 1,000) halves; sensitivity and specificity were computed separately within each half at the calibrated threshold (see Multimedia Appendix 1 section F). For operating point analysis, we identified decision thresholds on the development set achieving target specificity levels, then evaluated sensitivity on the held-out test set. Stratified evaluations by hazard type and equity analyses by sex and race/ethnicity were performed; subgroup results should be interpreted with caution given sample size limitations (see Multimedia Appendix 1 section G). All analyses used R 4.3.1 and Python 3.11.

For equity analyses, the primary multiple-comparison correction is Benjamini-Hochberg FDR (q<0.05), which is more appropriate than FWER control when missing true disparities carries public health cost. Hochberg FWER control is also reported as a sensitivity check. All subgroup comparisons yielded q>.05 under BH-FDR and P>.05 under Hochberg correction, with the caveat that statistical power was limited (n_hazard ≤93 per subgroup). We do not interpret non-significance as evidence of equitable performance; we report point estimates with 95% Wilson CIs and note that wider CIs in smaller subgroups reflect limited power rather than demonstrated equivalence.

### Patient and Public Involvement

No patients were involved in setting the research question, outcome measures, study design, or interpretation of results. Results will be disseminated to participating care management organizations to inform deployment decisions.

## RESULTS

### Linguistic Characteristics of Real-World Data

Real-world messages differed substantially from physician-created scenarios in linguistic structure (Table 1). Real-world messages averaged 18 words (IQR 12-26) versus 34 words (IQR 25-45) for physician scenarios (P<.001, Mann-Whitney U test). Real-world messages contained higher rates of colloquialisms (1,410/3,000, 47% vs 15/200, 8%), abbreviations (930/3,000, 31% vs 24/200, 12%), and implicit contextual references (690/3,000, 23% vs 9/200, 5%; all P<.001, chi-square tests). Real-world messages averaged grade 6.2 reading level (Flesch-Kincaid) versus grade 11.4 for physician scenarios (P<.001). ### Hazard Detection Performance: Physician Test Set

On physician-created scenarios (n=41, 61% hazard prevalence), all architectures achieved strong performance with wide confidence intervals due to small sample size (Table S1 in Multimedia Appendix 1). The CQL decision controller demonstrated sensitivity 0.96 (95% CI 0.80–0.99) and specificity 0.94 (95% CI 0.70–0.99). The constellation architecture achieved sensitivity 0.92 (95% CI 0.74–0.98) and specificity 0.88 (95% CI 0.62–0.97). The safety-augmented GPT-5.1 showed sensitivity 0.74 (95% CI 0.54–0.87) and specificity 0.94 (95% CI 0.70–0.99). Given the small sample size (N=41) and high hazard prevalence (61%), confidence intervals for the physician test set are very wide and between-architecture differences are largely non-informative; the physician set serves primarily as a distribution-shift reference rather than a primary evaluation dataset.

Temporal stability: To assess approximate temporal stability without exact timestamps, we conducted a quasi-prospective sensitivity analysis. The 2,000 test messages were sorted lexicographically by CUID identifier (encoding approximate creation timestamp) and divided into a chronologically-earlier half (first 1,000 messages; n_hazard=87, 8.7% prevalence) and a chronologically-later half (last 1,000 messages; n_hazard=78, 7.8% prevalence). Results were stable: across all six architectures with calibrated probability scores, the maximum early-to-late sensitivity change was 10.4 percentage points (logistic regression: early 0.345, late 0.449); five of six architectures showed changes ≤5 percentage points (guardrails: early 0.598, late 0.615; constellation: early 0.701, late 0.705; XGBoost: early 0.402, late 0.449; CQL-reward: early 0.632, late 0.654; TinyLlama: early 0.391, late 0.359). Critically, no architecture showed a monotonic deterioration from earlier to later messages — the directional pattern was non-monotonic for all six, providing no evidence of systematic temporal drift over the January 2023–November 2025 study period. Full stratified results are in Multimedia Appendix 1 Table S6. This analysis is limited by CUID lexicographic ordering as an approximation of chronological order and cannot substitute for a prospective temporal validation. Because models use only text features without timestamps, training-test chronological overlap (due to random rather than temporal splitting) represents a generalizability limitation rather than data leakage.

### Few-Shot Prompting, Fine-Tuning, and DeepSeek Subset Evaluation

To assess whether additional examples could improve LLM performance and to characterize LLM operating points through prompt-based tuning (a proxy for log-probability threshold tuning), we evaluated GPT-5.1 with 0, 5, and 10-shot prompting on a stratified 500-message subset (41 hazards, 8.25% prevalence; Figure S2). These experiments demonstrate the sensitivity-specificity tradeoff achievable through prompt engineering: zero-shot GPT-5.1 (safety-augmented, subset) achieved sensitivity 0.244 (95% CI 0.138–0.393) and specificity 0.959 (95% CI 0.936–0.974); 5-shot prompting shifted the operating point to sensitivity 0.537 (95% CI 0.380–0.689) and specificity 0.486 (95% CI 0.438–0.534); 10-shot prompting achieved sensitivity 0.610 (95% CI 0.449–0.753) with specificity collapsing to 0.495 (95% CI 0.447–0.542), approximately a 50% false alarm rate. This systematic operating-point progression demonstrates that prompt engineering can shift LLM operating points substantially — but even 10-shot tuned GPT-5.1 (sensitivity 0.610 at specificity 0.495) does not match local supervised models at the matched specificity of 0.73 (local sensitivity range 0.570–0.715 at specificity=0.73, Table 3).

Fine-tuned Llama-1.1B [41] achieved 0.376 sensitivity (95% CI 0.305–0.453) with 0.774 specificity and AUROC 0.701 on the full 2,000-message test set. Default-threshold sensitivity remained below all supervised architectures; however, fine-tuned Llama's AUROC (0.701) exceeded both GPT-5.1 configurations (0.617–0.651), indicating that domain-specific fine-tuning improved the shape of the operating curve even without improving default-threshold sensitivity.

DeepSeek-R1 evaluated on the complete 2,000-message test set: DeepSeek-R1 [33], deployed locally via Ollama (deepseek-r1:latest), was evaluated on all 2,000 real-world test messages using a safety-augmented prompt. DeepSeek-R1 achieved sensitivity 0.224 (37/165 hazards detected; 95% CI 0.163–0.290) and specificity 0.866 (95% CI 0.851–0.882). False negatives occurred at a rate of 64.0 per 1,000 messages. This compares to GPT-5.1 zero-shot (safety-augmented): sensitivity 0.279 (95% CI 0.216–0.352), specificity 0.954 (95% CI 0.944–0.963). DeepSeek-R1 achieved higher sensitivity than zero-shot GPT-5.1 but lower specificity, consistent with a different default operating point. AUROC was not computed for DeepSeek-R1, as it does not provide calibrated probability scores at inference.

### Performance Degradation Analysis

LLM systems demonstrated substantial performance reduction when transitioning from physician scenarios to real-world messages (Figure 1): GPT-5.1 (safety-augmented) declined 34 percentage points in sensitivity (0.74 to 0.40; 95% CI for difference 0.25-0.43; P<.001 by McNemar test). In contrast, decision-theoretic and constellation architectures declined 23-24 points. All pairwise comparisons of LLM degradation versus complex safety architecture degradation achieved P<.001 (Hochberg-corrected McNemar tests). Because cross-set comparisons are substantially confounded by hazard prevalence (61% physician vs 8.25% real-world) and sample size (N=41 vs N=2,000), the primary cross-set comparison metric is AUROC, which is threshold-independent and prevalence-insensitive; absolute sensitivity comparisons across sets are reported as exploratory only.

### Equity Analysis

Among the 1,536 messages with available demographic data (77% of test set), the CQL controller (sensitivity-optimized) achieved sensitivity 0.753 (95% CI 0.656–0.829) in female patients (n=1,013, n_hazard=93) and 0.783 (95% CI 0.644–0.877) in male patients (n=523, n_hazard=46). Both values exceeded the full-test-set sensitivity (0.727), indicating the demographic-available subset had higher-than-average detection rates; the pairwise female-male difference of 3 percentage points was not statistically significant (P=.66). The 23% of patients lacking demographic data (n=464, n_hazard=26) had an estimated sensitivity of approximately 0.54, suggesting patients without linked demographic records may have systematically different message characteristics. By race/ethnicity (among groups with n_hazard ≥10): White 0.778 (95% CI 0.651–0.868, n_hazard=54), Black/African American 0.768 (95% CI 0.656–0.852, n_hazard=69), and Hispanic 0.800 (95% CI 0.490–0.943, n_hazard=10) showed similar and overlapping sensitivity; the equalized odds difference in sensitivity across these groups was 0.047. The false positive rate was higher in female patients (FPR 0.382) than male (FPR 0.306), an equalized odds difference of 0.076 indicating a greater false alarm burden for female patients. AIAN (n_hazard=1) and Asian (n_hazard=2) groups had insufficient hazard events for meaningful sensitivity estimates. Full stratified analyses with confidence intervals for all architectures are in Multimedia Appendix 1 section G.

Sensitivity analysis for missing demographic data: A Manski-style partial identification analysis characterized the plausible range of overall sensitivity under different assumptions about the missing-demographic group. If the 26 hazardous messages in the missing-demographic group were all detected (optimistic bound), overall sensitivity would be 0.800; if none were detected (conservative bound), overall sensitivity would be 0.642. The observed overall sensitivity (0.727) implies estimated sensitivity in the missing-demographic group of 0.538 (14/26 hazards detected), substantially below the demographic-available average (0.766).

## DISCUSSION

### Principal Findings

In this comparative evaluation using 2,000 real-world patient messages, architectures with explicit safety mechanisms substantially outperformed tested large language model configurations on hazard detection sensitivity, with a 30-35 percentage point gap at matched specificity operating points. However, which architecture leads depends on the metric of interest. The CQL controller (sensitivity-optimized) achieved the highest default-threshold sensitivity (0.727) through asymmetric reward optimization; the constellation architecture and rule-based guardrails led all configurations by AUROC (0.801 each), reflecting superior overall discrimination across the full operating curve. A third notable finding is that logistic regression with TF-IDF features achieved default-threshold sensitivity statistically equivalent to GPT-5.1 (safety-augmented) (0.394 vs 0.400; McNemar P>.99), yet achieved higher AUROC (0.716 vs 0.651). The safety-augmented system prompt improved GPT-5.1 sensitivity by 12 percentage points over the default prompt (0.400 vs 0.279) but at the cost of specificity (0.901 vs 0.954), demonstrating that prompt design functions as an operating point adjustment mechanism even in zero-shot settings. Together, these results suggest that the primary limitation of tested LLM configurations in this setting is the absence of domain-specific optimization pressure and supervised feature engineering — not discriminative ability per se. These findings reflect a single retrospective internal validation in a Medicaid care coordination program; generalizability to other settings, patient populations, or future LLM versions requires independent validation.

### Comparison to Prior Work

The linguistic characteristics analysis reveals important features of real-world patient communications. Patients use colloquialisms (47%), abbreviations (31%), and implicit context (23%) — linguistic patterns underrepresented in the medical literature and clinical vignettes that constitute much of LLM training data. This aligns with recent findings on RAG system limitations [11,25] and challenges in medical AI generalization [14,21]. The performance reduction we observed likely reflects a combination of linguistic distribution shift, architectural factors, and the explicit safety optimization of the local model pipelines — these contributions cannot be separated in the current study design.

The 3,000-message real-world corpus used in this study, with physician-adjudicated safety hazard labels for all messages, is substantially larger than most published AI triage validation corpora. Most comparative evaluations of AI clinical communication rely on standardized patient consultations or physician-generated scenarios rather than de-identified messages from routine care operations; the natural hazard prevalence (8.25%) and authentic linguistic variation in this dataset provide a more realistic test of deployment performance than enriched benchmarks.

The study population is drawn from a multistate Medicaid care coordination program serving over 80,000 enrolled beneficiaries annually. Medicaid covers more than 80 million Americans [40] — the largest single health insurance program in the United States — with disproportionate representation of racial and ethnic minorities, low-income households, individuals with disabilities, and those with complex medical and social needs. AI models validated primarily in academic medical center cohorts may generalize poorly to Medicaid and safety-net populations; validation in a Medicaid-serving program therefore addresses a representativeness gap in the existing evaluation literature. Generalizability is additionally limited by health literacy, dialectal variation, and population specificity. The studied population communicates predominantly in standard American English; performance on messages in Spanish, African American Vernacular English, or low-literacy communication styles was not specifically assessed. AI triage systems optimized for standard English text may demonstrate differential performance across language communities.

### Architectural Considerations

The AUROC and sensitivity results reveal a dissociation between two distinct notions of performance advantage. The constellation architecture and rule-based guardrails led all configurations by AUROC (0.801 each), indicating superior overall discriminative ability across operating thresholds, while the CQL controller (sensitivity-optimized) achieved the highest default-threshold sensitivity (0.727) through asymmetric reward optimization. The controller's AUROC (0.731) ranked fourth, behind constellation, guardrails, and XGBoost (0.760). This pattern has a straightforward mechanistic interpretation: the CQL reward function penalizes missed hazards 25× more than false alarms, which shifts the operating point toward high sensitivity at the cost of specificity, but does not improve the underlying operating curve. Constellation's 23 specialized per-category classifiers, each calibrated independently, produce better-calibrated probability estimates across thresholds — an advantage captured by AUROC but not by single-threshold sensitivity.

The CQL reward function penalizes missed hazards 25× more than false alarms, while LLM configurations received only natural-language safety instructions without equivalent optimization pressure. This reward asymmetry, rather than the RL architecture per se, likely contributes to the sensitivity gap. The safety-augmented GPT-5.1 prompt (which explicitly instructs the model to prioritize safety) achieves 12 percentage points higher sensitivity than the default prompt, consistent with prompt-based operating point adjustment. However, even the safety-augmented prompt achieves only 40% sensitivity, leaving a substantial gap versus the CQL controller (72.7%) that persists at matched specificity levels. The comparison of CQL to a fixed-threshold rule using the same upstream detector (Appendix C.4: threshold-optimized sensitivity 0.715 at specificity 0.730) indicates that the primary contribution of CQL over simple threshold-based classification is in action differentiation (9-action policy) rather than binary hazard detection per se.

The equivalence of logistic regression and GPT-5.1 (safety-augmented) at default thresholds (0.394 vs 0.400; McNemar P>.99, AUROC 0.716 vs 0.651) is a substantive finding. A simple bag-of-words classifier with transparent features matched a frontier LLM on sensitivity in this task, and outperformed it on overall discrimination. This suggests that the performance deficit of LLMs in this setting reflects the absence of domain-specific optimization pressure — supervised training signal for hazard categories, calibration against real-world prevalence, and asymmetric reward — rather than any inherent limitation of the underlying language representation. Consistent with this interpretation, fine-tuned Llama-1.1B achieved higher AUROC (0.701) than both GPT-5.1 configurations (0.617 and 0.651) after domain-specific training, even at lower default-threshold sensitivity than GPT-5.1 (safety-augmented).

These observations suggest that healthcare organizations deploying LLMs for triage safety should consider domain-specific fine-tuning, supervised calibration against local hazard prevalence, and explicit safety optimization as complementary adaptations that may substantially close the gap with local supervised pipelines — rather than accepting zero-shot prompted performance as the relevant comparison benchmark.

### Strengths and Limitations

This study is a formative evaluation: it provides preliminary real-world evidence on architectural performance tradeoffs to inform design of subsequent prospective validation studies, rather than establishing definitive clinical efficacy for any single architecture. Strengths include: (1) Scale and ecological validity — 3,000 authentic patient messages from routine Medicaid care coordination, substantially larger than most published comparative AI triage evaluations; (2) Population representativeness — Medicaid beneficiaries include a high proportion of racial and ethnic minorities, low-income households, and medically complex individuals; (3) Natural hazard prevalence — the 8.25% real-world hazard rate reflects actual clinical conditions; (4) Systematic head-to-head architecture comparison on identical test cases; (5) Comprehensive safety-specific metrics encompassing hazard detection, action appropriateness, and equity; and (6) Rigorous human ground truth from three board-certified physicians (Fleiss kappa 0.82).

Several important limitations warrant consideration. First, this is a single-program retrospective internal validation in Medicaid beneficiaries; linguistic patterns, hazard prevalence, and care workflows may differ substantially in other populations, payer types, or healthcare settings. These findings should not be generalized to AI triage broadly. Second, splitting was performed at the message level; 41 of 1,679 test patients (2.4%) also contributed to the development set, affecting 68 test messages (3.4%). Models used aggregate text features without patient identifiers, limiting the practical impact. Third, a formal temporal training split (training exclusively on messages from an earlier period and testing on a later holdout) was not performed; a post-hoc quasi-prospective temporal stability analysis demonstrated no systematic temporal drift over the study period (Results §Temporal Stability; Appendix Table S6), but this cannot substitute for prospective validation: novel linguistic patterns or hazard types emerging after the training period may not be captured. Fourth, ground truth reflects physician consensus rather than verified clinical outcomes. Fifth, the physician test set (N=41, 61% hazard prevalence) is substantially underpowered for between-architecture comparisons. Sixth, the performance advantage of local pipelines partly reflects multi-stage feature engineering (supervised classifier, calibration) and asymmetric reward optimization in addition to architectural factors. Seventh, 23% of patients lacked demographic data, limiting equity analyses; patients in the demographic-unavailable group had an estimated sensitivity approximately 17 percentage points lower than the demographic-available average (0.54 vs 0.77), suggesting patients without linked enrollment records may have systematically different message or care characteristics. Eighth, ground truth was established by physician consensus using unanimity (all three physicians must agree). While this maximizes specificity of hazard labels, it may systematically exclude borderline cases carrying genuine clinical uncertainty; if such cases are enriched among false negatives, sensitivity estimates may understate clinical utility. Ninth, all LLM evaluations used single-turn inference. Many deployed LLM triage assistants implement multi-turn dialogue in which the LLM requests clarification before recommending action; single-turn evaluation likely underestimates LLM performance in multi-turn deployments. Tenth, full RAG evaluation (integrating retrieved clinical guidelines into LLM inference) on the primary test set was not completed in this version of the study; both are identified as Future Work priorities.

### Clinical and Policy Implications

Healthcare organizations implementing automated triage systems should: validate systems on actual patient communications before deployment; consider multi-stage architectures with explicit hazard detection rather than relying solely on LLM prompting; implement continuous monitoring for performance degradation; establish tiered alert thresholds balancing sensitivity and false alarm burden; and conduct prospective fairness analyses. At the observed operating point of the best-performing system (PPV 0.194, sensitivity 0.727), approximately 4 false alarms accompany each confirmed hazard (1/PPV − 1 = 4.15), requiring clinical review of approximately 5 total alerts per true hazard; organizations should assess whether staffing can sustain this alert volume before deployment. Regulatory frameworks may appropriately require demonstration of performance on actual patient communications before medical AI deployment.

Healthcare organizations should also account for the substantial development, validation, and maintenance costs of local supervised models. Local pipeline architectures require: annotated training data (this study used 570 labeled examples assembled over approximately 6 months); clinical expert time for adjudication (three board-certified physicians); infrastructure for model serving, monitoring, and retraining; and regulatory validation documentation. These hidden costs must be weighed against the performance advantages reported here. In contrast, LLM APIs require minimal upfront development but introduce vendor dependency, version change risk, and ongoing API costs ($184 for this evaluation). Organizations may find that domain-specific supervised calibration of LLMs — requiring less labeled data than full pipeline development — provides an intermediate option.

### Future Work

A direct preference optimization (DPO) variant of Llama-1.1B trained with preference pairs derived from the same asymmetric reward function used for CQL (rewarding hazard detection, penalizing missed hazards 25× more than false alarms) would provide the most methodologically rigorous comparison of the RL-specific contribution to performance, following the protocol of Rafailov et al. (NeurIPS 2023) [42]. DPO training with 570 labeled examples is computationally feasible on a single GPU within approximately 4 hours using the current training infrastructure.

Future studies should implement temporal data splitting to assess concept drift and assess RAG augmentation on the primary test set (the clinical guideline corpus and retrieval infrastructure are already built; evaluation was deferred for this revision). Future evaluations should obtain calibrated log-probability outputs from LLMs to enable threshold-based operating point tuning equivalent to that applied for local supervised models. Single-turn evaluation may underestimate the performance of LLMs in contexts where they can request clarification; future evaluations should assess multi-turn dialogue architectures.

### Conclusions

In this formative single-program retrospective internal validation, local supervised architectures substantially outperformed tested large language model configurations under real-world deployment conditions, exceeding zero-shot LLMs by 30-35 percentage points in sensitivity at matched operating points. Performance advantages were metric-specific: the CQL controller (sensitivity-optimized) achieved highest sensitivity through asymmetric reward optimization, while constellation and rule-based guardrail architectures led all configurations on overall discrimination (AUROC 0.801 each). Logistic regression with domain-specific features demonstrated sensitivity equivalent to GPT-5.1 (safety-augmented), and safety-augmented prompting improved LLM sensitivity by 12 percentage points over default prompting — consistent with prompt engineering as an operating point adjustment mechanism. Domain-specific fine-tuning improved Llama-1.1B's operating curve discrimination, suggesting that supervised calibration and optimization pressure, more than architectural category per se, drive the performance gap. Real-world validation using authentic patient communications, with reporting of both sensitivity and full operating curve metrics, should be standard practice before deploying AI triage systems. Generalizability to other populations, settings, and LLM versions requires independent validation.

Patient message data cannot be shared due to HIPAA regulations. Code and evaluation scripts are available at the project repository [37].

## FIGURE CAPTIONS

**Figure 1. Performance degradation and sensitivity-specificity tradeoff across nine AI architectures.**

Panel A (left): Sensitivity decline (percentage points) from physician-created scenarios to real-world patient messages for seven architectures (CQL controller variants shown combined; few-shot and DeepSeek subset evaluations excluded from this panel). Bars represent the absolute difference in sensitivity between the two test sets; architectures are ordered from least to greatest degradation. Panel B (right): Sensitivity-specificity operating points on both test sets. Triangles represent performance on the physician-created test set (n=41, 61% hazard prevalence); circles represent performance on the real-world test set (n=2,000, 8.25% hazard prevalence). Lines connect each architecture's physician and real-world operating points. The shaded region indicates the clinically plausible operating range (sensitivity ≥70% and specificity ≥70%). Architecture colors are consistent across both panels. CQL = conservative Q-learning decision controller.

**Figure 2. Action recommendation appropriateness across AI architectures (real-world test set).**

Stacked horizontal bar chart showing the proportion of test messages classified as appropriate action (green), under-triage (red), and over-triage (orange) for each architecture. The dashed vertical line indicates the 70% appropriate-action clinical target. Results shown for all nine primary configurations (n=2,000 each) plus DeepSeek-R1 (n=2,000) and GPT-5.1 few-shot variants (0-shot, 5-shot, 10-shot) evaluated on the 500-message subset. For the CQL action pipeline, the ActionHead component is shown (purpose-trained for 9-class action classification); the CQL controller's binary hazard-to-action mapping (reward-optimized variant) is also shown at 63.1%. Under-triage (system recommends less urgent action than ground truth) represents the most consequential failure mode for patient safety. The decision-theoretic pipeline's action recommendation component (ActionHead) achieved the highest appropriate-action rate (77.7%). Fine-tuned Llama-1.1B achieved 69.6% with notably low under-triage (8.6%), comparable to supervised classifiers. LLM approaches showed high over-triage rates (GPT-5.1 safety 69.1%; GPT-5.1 default 69.9%), reflecting a tendency to escalate benign messages inappropriately. CQL = conservative Q-learning.
